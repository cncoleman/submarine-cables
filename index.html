Network analysis · submarine cables

The internet has a shape. Most of it is underwater.

Around 500 fiber-optic cables carry nearly all intercontinental traffic across the seafloor. Drawn as a graph — landing stations as nodes, shared cables as edges — the network reveals a hierarchy the map alone hides: the places with the most cables are rarely the places the network can least afford to lose.

01

Two ways to be important

Each country gets two scores. Connectivity counts how many other countries it shares a cable with — raw presence in the network. Betweenness measures how many of all country-to-country shortest paths run through it — its role as a relay. Sorted by betweenness, the gap between the bars is the whole story.

Distinct country connections Betweenness centrality × 100
The United States has betweenness 0.43, more than triple any other country, while France, Egypt and India rank high on connectivity but low on transit.

The United States sits on 43% of all inter-country shortest paths — a single point of transit dominance unmatched anywhere else. France, Egypt, India and Italy show the inverse: dense connectivity, modest transit. They are destinations, not corridors.

02

The busiest corridors

Where connectivity concentrates: country pairs ranked by the number of distinct cables running between them. The Red Sea / Suez land bridge (Egypt) and the Singapore–Indonesia strait recur — the network's true pinch points.

Egypt appears in three of the top corridors — every Europe-to-Asia route funnels through the Suez land crossing, the most consequential chokepoint on the planet for internet traffic.

Built in waves

Cable systems by ready-for-service year. Two booms stand out: the dot-com build-out around 2000, and the current content-provider era — Google, Meta and Amazon now fund a large share of new capacity, pushing 2022–2023 to record counts.

05

What breaks first

A failure simulation. Starting from the intact graph, we pull out countries one at a time and measure the largest surviving connected cluster — the share of landing points that can still reach each other. Removing the highest-betweenness countries tears the network apart; removing the same number at random barely scratches it. That gap is what "critical infrastructure" means, quantified.

Targeted removal (by betweenness) Random removal
Removing the 15 highest-betweenness countries shrinks the largest cluster from 71 percent to 34 percent of landing points, while random removal leaves it near 68 percent.
0 removed

The cliff at step 10 is Brazil — specifically Fortaleza, the South Atlantic's pivot. Once it goes, the Americas–Africa–Europe paths that routed through it have nowhere to reconverge, and the giant cluster drops from 51% to 38% in a single removal.

Reproducible

Built from TeleGeography's public GeoJSON API. The graph treats every landing point on a cable as connected to every other; country-level edges aggregate those links. Betweenness computed on the largest connected component (955 of 1,335 nodes).
